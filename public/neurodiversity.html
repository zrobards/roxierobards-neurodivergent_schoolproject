Foundations

Neurodiversity respects that brains learn, sense, and communicate in many ways.

It is a perspective about human variation, not a diagnosis. This page offers brief, careful overviews to build understanding.

Definition

Neurodiversity describes the natural range of ways people think, process information, move, and interact. Neurodivergent simply means a person whose brain processes the world differently from what is considered typical. Respecting neurodiversity means assuming capability and valuing different needs.

ADHD

Attention patterns can shift quickly. Energy may come in bursts. Supports like clear steps, interest-based tasks, and movement breaks can help.

Autism

Differences in social communication and sensory processing. Predictable routines, plain language, and respecting sensory needs make spaces more comfortable.

Dyslexia

Differences in reading and language processing. Audiobooks, clear fonts, and multimodal learning (visual, audio, hands-on) reduce barriers.

Sensory Processing

Sensory input (sound, light, texture, movement) may feel extra intense or muted. Offering choice in seating, lighting, and noise levels supports comfort.

OCD (brief overview)

OCD involves intrusive thoughts and repetitive responses that feel hard to stop. It is not a personality trait. Compassion, space, and professional support can help.

Strengths often seen

  • Creativity and original problem-solving
  • Deep focus on areas of interest
  • Pattern recognition and detail spotting
  • Honest communication and loyalty

Common challenges

  • Sensory overload or fatigue in busy spaces
  • Executive function hurdles like organizing and pacing
  • Social misunderstandings or stigma
  • Access barriers when environments are inflexible

Myths vs Facts

Myth

Neurodiversity is only about one condition.

Fact

It is an umbrella idea that includes many neurodevelopmental differences.

Myth

Accommodations are unfair advantages.

Fact

Accommodations remove barriers so people can access learning and participation fairly.

Myth

Neurodivergent people cannot thrive at school.

Fact

With respect, flexibility, and support, neurodivergent students succeed and enrich communities.
